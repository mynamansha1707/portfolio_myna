How I Think

Product Instinct
What's broken about how most AI tools handle calendar scheduling?
They optimize for slot-finding, not intent. The real friction isn't "when are you free" — it's "what kind of meeting is this and does it need to happen at all." An AI agent that asks better questions upstream would reduce calendar load by 30% before touching availability. The scheduling feature is a band-aid on a prioritization problem.
First Principles
Why do most enterprise analytics dashboards go unused after launch?
Because they're built around data availability, not decisions. The right starting question is: "what would you do differently if you knew X?" — and work backwards to metrics. At NALCO, I learned this firsthand. The ops team didn't want more data. They wanted one number that told them whether to act today.
Design Sensibility
What's a UX failure you think about often?
Meeting recap tools that dump a wall of AI-generated text. The format misunderstands the use case — after a 60-minute call, nobody reads 800 words. The output should be three bullets + one action item, defaulting to what changes because of this meeting. Information hierarchy is a product decision, not a formatting one.
Ownership Mindset
How do you handle ambiguity without a PM to unblock you?
I write down the decision I'm about to make, the assumption it rests on, and the cheapest way to test that assumption. Usually the answer is a 20-minute user conversation or a quick data pull — not a meeting. Ambiguity isn't blocked; it's an invitation to define the problem better than anyone else has yet.
